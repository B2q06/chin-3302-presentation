# 中国股市 (Zhōngguó Gǔshì) — Presentation Script

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title
**中国股市 (Zhōngguó Gǔshì) — China's Stock Market**

> "Today we're going to talk about the Chinese stock market — but not just the numbers. We're going to look at the *culture* behind it. The gambling, the memes, the superstitions, and why 200 million retail investors — 散户 (sànhù) — are what they call 炒股 (chǎo gǔ), literally 'stir-frying' their life savings."

---

## SLIDE 2 — Value Started with Shells
**贝 (Bèi) — The Root of Wealth**

> "Before we get to the modern market, we need to understand where the concept of value even started in China. It started with cowrie shells — 贝 (bèi). This is one of the oldest Chinese characters, and it's embedded in every word related to money: 财 (cái, wealth), 贸 (mào, trade), 货 (huò, goods), 贵 (guì, precious), 购 (gòu, buy). The breakthrough here was separating *utility* from *value* — a shell isn't useful, but everyone agreed it was worth something. That shared agreement is the foundation of any market."

---

## SLIDE 3 — The Middle Man Concept

> "Here's the key insight: we don't actually trade for money. We trade for the goods and services money represents. Money is just a middleman — a temporary storage for value. Good to currency to good. Shells became coins, coins became paper, and paper became stocks. Each evolution is just a more abstract version of the same middleman. Stocks are just the latest version — a middleman for *ownership*."

---

## SLIDE 4 — Qin & Han: The Standardized Rules
**半两 (Bàn Liǎng) — 221 BCE**

> "When Qin Shi Huang unified China in 221 BCE, one of the first things he did was standardize the currency. The 半两 (bàn liǎng) coin — 'half a tael' — was circular with a square hole. Circle represents heaven, square represents earth. One currency for the entire empire. Think of this as the ancient equivalent of establishing listing rules for a stock exchange — standardized valuation, centralized rules. The friction was that metal is heavy. You can't easily move it long distances."

---

## SLIDE 5 — Tang Dynasty: Flying Money
**飞钱 (Fēiqián) — 618–907 CE**

> "That problem gets solved in the Tang Dynasty with 飞钱 (fēiqián) — 'flying money.' You deposit your cash in the capital, get a paper voucher, and redeem it in your home province. This was an early bill of exchange. The conceptual leap is huge: value starts traveling as *information*, not as physical material. That DNA — value as information — is the foundation of every stock exchange that exists today."

---

## SLIDE 6 — The Jiaozi: World's First "Stock"
**交子 (Jiāozǐ) — ~1024 CE, Song Dynasty**

> "Then in the Song Dynasty, around 1024, there's a metal shortage in Sichuan. Private merchants start issuing paper receipts — 交子 (jiāozǐ) — backed by iron and copper reserves. The key moment is when people start trading the *receipts* instead of the metal. When you trade the promise instead of the thing, you have a stock market in embryo. China invented paper money 600 years before Europe. This is the world's first paper currency."

---

## SLIDE 7 — 1990: The Sleeping Giant Awakes
**上交所 (Shàng Jiāo Suǒ) & 深交所 (Shēn Jiāo Suǒ)**

> "Fast forward to 1990. The Shanghai Stock Exchange — 上交所 (shàng jiāo suǒ) — opens December 19. The Shenzhen Stock Exchange — 深交所 (shēn jiāo suǒ) — opens December 1. A thousand years of 'paper promise' history meets modern technology. But here's the contrast that matters: in the US, institutions dominate. In China, it's *retail* — individual investors — who drive everything."

---

## SLIDE 8 — Transition: The Culture
**Part 02**

> "So the structure exists. But what about the *culture*? Why do 220 million Chinese retail investors behave so differently? That's where it gets really interesting. We're going to learn some key vocabulary: 韭菜 (jiǔcài), 主力 (zhǔlì), 股吧 (gǔ bā), 关灯吃面 (guān dēng chī miàn), 大V (dà V), 国家队 (guójiā duì), 风水 (fēngshuǐ)."

---

## SLIDE 9 — Who's Actually Trading?

> "Look at this table. In the US, retail investors make up about 20% of daily trading volume. In China's A-share market, it's 80 to 90%. There are 220 million retail accounts — 99.6% of all accounts. The average holding period is just 40 days, compared to 90 in the US. And instead of following Goldman Sachs or BlackRock, Chinese retail investors follow forum influencers — 大V (dà V) — and 'stock gods,' and government signals. There's also almost no short selling, which means there's no natural correction mechanism when stocks get overvalued."

---

## SLIDE 10 — The Leek Metaphor
**韭菜 (Jiǔcài)**

> "This is the single most important word in Chinese stock market culture: 韭菜 (jiǔcài) — leeks, or chives. It's what retail investors call *themselves*. Why? Because leeks regrow after you cut them — just like retail investors who lose everything, refill their accounts, and come right back. The phrase 割韭菜 (gē jiǔcài) — 'to harvest the leeks' — is what big money does to small investors. In the 2015 crash, the bottom 85% of households lost 250 billion RMB — 30% of their equity wealth. The top 0.5% gained. The leeks got harvested."

---

## SLIDE 11 — It's Not "Investing." It's Stir-Frying.
**炒股 (Chǎo Gǔ)**

> "In Chinese, you don't 'invest' in stocks. You 炒股 (chǎo gǔ) — you *stir-fry* stocks. High heat, rapid tossing. The whole vocabulary reflects this:
> - 上车/下车 (shàng chē / xià chē) — 'board the car / exit the car' — are you on the rocket or not?
> - 满仓 (mǎn cāng) — 'full position' — 100% all-in. The Chinese YOLO.
> - 割肉 (gē ròu) — 'cut the meat' — selling at a painful loss.
> - 套牢 (tào láo) — 'trapped' — stuck holding a stock that keeps falling.
> - 站岗 (zhàn gǎng) — 'standing guard' — holding a dead stock like a soldier at a post.
> - 坐轿/抬轿 (zuò jiào / tái jiào) — 'ride the sedan chair / carry the sedan chair' — early investors ride, late investors carry.
>
> And here's a stat: 65% of new investors in 2015 had never finished high school."

---

## SLIDE 12 — The 2015 Crash
**关灯吃面 (Guān Dēng Chī Miàn) — "Lights Off, Noodles"**

> "In 2015, the Shanghai index fell 43% in under 3 months. 30 million new brokerage accounts had been opened in just the first 5 months of that year — the government, through People's Daily, had been *urging* citizens to invest. They literally recruited the leeks. A farmer borrowed over a million dollars on margin because his broker told him to. He lost everything. CNBC filmed him. A 28-year-old tried to throw himself in front of a train after losing his savings plus 100,000 yuan he'd borrowed without telling his wife.
>
> The phrase 关灯吃面 (guān dēng chī miàn) — 'lights off, noodles' — was born here. A retail investor posted on Guba that he'd lost so much he was eating instant ramen alone in the dark. It became the canonical meme of Chinese stock market loss. It never went away.
>
> They also tried circuit breakers in January 2016. Abandoned after *two trading days* — instead of stopping panic, the circuit breakers caused a stampede."

---

## SLIDE 13 — 股吧 (Gǔ Bā): China's WallStreetBets

> "股吧 (gǔ bā) is hosted at guba.eastmoney.com — it's per-stock bulletin boards attached to a live brokerage. Every stock has its own 'bar' — 吧 (bā). No upvotes — posts are sorted by recency. 30,000 views makes something viral. BlackRock tracks 100,000 posts per day for sentiment signals.
>
> Research has shown that forum sentiment *statistically predicts* next-day returns for small and mid-cap stocks.
>
> There are also paid shills — 托儿 (tuō er) — who post coordinated bullish content to pump small-caps, then dump.
>
> Look at these actual quotes:
> - Hikvision forum: '2800亿的公司被我买崩三年' (2800 yì de gōngsī bèi wǒ mǎi bēng sān nián) — 'I collapsed a 280-billion-yuan company over 3 years.'
> - SAIC Motor forum: '上汽把我从结婚炒到离婚' (shàngqì bǎ wǒ cóng jiéhūn chǎo dào líhūn) — 'SAIC took me from marriage to divorce.'
> - Ping An Bank forum: '狗都不要的东西，我可以吗？' (gǒu dōu bù yào de dōngxi, wǒ kěyǐ ma?) — 'Even dogs wouldn't want it — can I?'"

---

## SLIDE 14 — Real Guba Posts: The Voice of 220M Retail Investors

> "Here are actual translated posts from 股吧 (gǔ bā):
>
> - Wanfeng Forum: '哭了一天了，眼睛肿的睁不开' (kū le yī tiān le, yǎnjing zhǒng de zhēng bù kāi) — 'Cried all day. Eyes too swollen to open.'
> - SSE Index Forum: '今晚无眠——关于明天的A股，最后几点提醒' (jīnwǎn wú mián — guānyú míngtiān de A gǔ, zuìhòu jǐ diǎn tíxǐng) — 'Tonight sleepless — regarding tomorrow's A-shares, a few final reminders.'
> - iFlytek Forum: '利好越大，跌得越狠' (lìhǎo yuè dà, diē de yuè hěn) — 'The bigger the good news, the worse it falls.'
> - Universal: '是龙你就盘着，是狗你就趴着，是垃圾你就臭着' (shì lóng nǐ jiù pán zhe, shì gǒu nǐ jiù pā zhe, shì lājī nǐ jiù chòu zhe) — 'If you're a dragon, coil. If a dog, lie down. If trash — stay putrid.'
>
> This is the voice of the market. These aren't jokes — these are real people losing real money."

---

## SLIDE 15 — China's "Wall Street": The 大V (Dà V)

> "In the US, 'smart money' follows Goldman, BlackRock, the Fed. In China, retail investors follow 大V (dà V) — forum influencers and 'stock god' fund managers. Tips come from Weibo, 雪球 (xuěqiú) — that's xueqiu.com, which has 57 million users — 股吧 (gǔ bā), and WeChat groups.
>
> Xueqiu has a copy-trading notification system — 57 million users get real-time alerts when big traders make moves.
>
> FactSet actually sells 'Guba Analytics' and 'Xueqiu Analytics' as institutional products. That's proof that Chinese retail sentiment literally moves markets."

---

## SLIDE 16 — Xu Xiang: The Stock God Who Was Robbing You
**徐翔 (Xú Xiāng) — 私募一哥 (Sīmù Yī Gē, "Hedge Fund Brother No. 1")**

> "This is 徐翔 (xú xiāng). He started trading at age 17, skipped college, used his parents' money. By 2015 he had a 3,000% return since 2010. Retail investors on 股吧 (gǔ bā) worshipped him. His trades were religiously copied.
>
> The reality? He was conspiring with management of 13 listed companies *simultaneously*. He'd time their announcements to match his buy orders, then dump on his followers.
>
> In November 2015, police sealed off the entire 35-kilometer Hangzhou Bay Bridge — 杭州湾大桥 (hángzhōu wān dà qiáo) — to arrest him in his car. He was photographed in handcuffs wearing an Armani suit. The fine was 11 billion yuan — the largest individual white-collar fine in Chinese history.
>
> The man retail investors copied as a 'stock god' had been systematically harvesting them as leeks the entire time."

## SLIDE 18 — The National Team
**国家队 (Guójiā Duì)**

> "In the US, the government regulates the market. In China, the government is the market's *biggest whale*. 国家队 (guójiā duì) — the 'National Team' — are state-backed funds that directly buy stocks to prop up the market. Central Huijin, China Securities Finance Corp, the National Social Security Fund.
>
> During the 2015 crash, they bought positions in over 1,400 companies to stop the freefall. In January 2024, they poured 17 billion dollars into just four CSI 300 ETFs in one month.
>
> Research has shown that detecting the national team's footprint was *more profitable* than reading quarterly earnings. The market is explicitly a tool of state economic policy — not a free capital market."

## SLIDE 19 — Chinese Stock Market Astrology
**风水 (Fēngshuǐ), 八字 (Bāzì), 主力 (Zhǔlì), 庄家 (Zhuāngjiā), 缠论 (Chán Lùn)**

> "Now for the wild stuff.
>
> 风水 (fēngshuǐ) — Feng Shui Indices. CLSA, a major brokerage, has published an annual Feng Shui stock market index since 1992. Their 2025 prediction: 'Bitcoin will suffer — the Snake year lacks metal and coins in the 八字 (bāzì) chart.' Two brokerages actually got official warnings from the CSRC — China's SEC — for publishing feng shui research.
>
> Lucky 8 — 八 (bā) sounds like 发 (fā, prosper). Unlucky 4 — 四 (sì) sounds like 死 (sǐ, death). 26% of Chinese IPO prices end in 8. That's versus the 10% you'd expect randomly. Alibaba's IPO price? $68. Ticker? BABA. The Beijing Olympics started at 8:08:08 PM on 08/08/08.
>
> 主力 (zhǔlì) — 'Main Force' Theory. Retail investors believe every stock move is orchestrated by a hidden big player. They read K-line candlestick patterns to try to decode what the 主力 is doing.
>
> 庄家 (zhuāngjiā) — the 'house' or 'banker.' There are courses sold for 6,800 yuan on how to ride manipulated stocks. Over 200 books on Amazon China. The saying goes: '你喝汤，他吃肉' (nǐ hē tāng, tā chī ròu) — 'You get the soup, he gets the meat.'
>
> 缠论 (chán lùn) — in 2006, an anonymous blogger mixed fractal geometry with Zen Buddhism into a trading system. Identity still unknown. Has a GitHub repo, a podcast series called '108 Lessons' — 108 is a sacred Buddhist number — and a devoted cult following.
>
> And finally: in China, red means UP and green means DOWN. The exact opposite of every Western market. Red is the color of luck — 红 (hóng). Chinese investors trained themselves to love the color that signals danger to everyone else."

## SLIDE 20 — Why Does China's Market Feel Like a Casino?

> "So why does it feel like a casino? Four reasons:
>
> **Structure**: Retail drives 80–90% of volume. No short sellers to correct overvaluation. Daily trading limits trap investors in bad positions.
>
> **Culture**: 炒股 (chǎo gǔ) — stir-fry stocks. 韭菜 (jiǔcài) — leeks. Gambling is culturally normalized. The market is seen as fate and luck, not fundamentals.
>
> **Information**: There's no independent Wall Street. Retail investors follow forum 大V (dà V), 'stock gods,' and government signals instead of earnings reports.
>
> **The State**: The government is both the regulator AND the biggest whale. The market is a policy tool, not a free capital market.
>
> 220 million leeks. One very large pair of scissors — 一把大剪刀 (yī bǎ dà jiǎndāo). 谢谢 (xièxiè) — thank you."
